Designing a System of Linear Equations

How exactly is linear algebra a key component in many optimization and statistics problems? The answer is solving the title of this section. In most optimization problems, we want to find the best way to allocate resources to accomplish a goal. Not the largest/smallest nor longest/shortest way, but optimal. Deriving from an example in (Yoshida 2021), a freight rail company wants to transport commodities \((x_1, x_2, x_3)\) from one place to another. However, they can’t transport the entire inventory of each commodity at once due to constraints:

  • Commodities \(x_1, x_2,\) and \(x_3\) weigh 1, 2, and 3 tons respectively as the weight capacity is 1,250 tons.
  • Commodities \(x_1, x_2,\) and \(x_3\) require 2, 1, and 4 freight cars each with 1,800 freight cars available.
  • Commodities \(x_2\) and \(x_3\) must add to 300 units respectively.

\[\begin{align} \left[\begin{array}{@{}ccc|c@{}} 1 & 2 & 3 & 1250 \\ 2 & 1 & 4 & 1800 \\ 0 & 1 & 1 & 300 \end{array}\right] \leftrightarrow \left[\begin{array}{@{}ccc|c@{}} x_1 & 2x_3 & 3x_3 & 1250 \\ 2x_1 & x_2 & 4x_3 & 1800 \\ & x_2 & x_3 & 300 \end{array}\right] \end{align}\]

On the left-hand side (LHS), you have your coefficient matrix which are made up of the unit values from your commodities. \(x_1, x_2, x_3\) are referred to as decision variables since we’re trying to figure out the quantity needed to fulfill our linear equations. On the right-hand side (RHS), we have our constraints which are our limitations or restrictions.

Solving a System of Linear Equations

Normally, this problem set would be solved through gaussian elimination and back substitution. Basically, you’re elimininating unknown variables by manipulating equations. For example, if I wanted to get rid of \(x_1\) in the 2nd equation, replace the 2nd equation by the sum of multiplying the 1st equation by -2 and the 2nd equation itself. You do this repeatedly until an equation has a single unknown.

\[\begin{align} \left[\begin{array}{@{}ccc|c@{}} -2 & -4 & -6 & -2500 \\ 2 & 1 & 4 & 1800 \\ \hline 0 & -3 & -2 & -700 \end{array}\right] \rightarrow \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 & 150 \\ 0 & -3 & -2 & -700 \\ 0 & 1 & 1 & 300 \end{bmatrix} \rightarrow \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 & 150 \\ 0 & -3 & -2 & -700 \\ 0 & 0 & 3 & 900 \end{bmatrix} \end{align}\]

\(x_3\) results in 300 when dividing the constraint by the coefficient of commodity \(x_3\). For the back subtitution, plug in 300 into \(x_3\) in equation 2 to solve for \(x_2\) resulting as 100. Plug both values into their respective values in equation 1 to get 450 for \(x_1\). As a result, \((x_1, x_2, x_3) = (450, 100, 200)\). However, as these systems of linear equations begin to scale in size, you could imagine that it isn’t practical to solve them by hand. That’s where Python comes in.

import numpy as np

A = np.array([[1, 2, 3],
              [2, 1, 4],
              [0, 1, 1]])
              
b = np.array([1250, 1800, 300])

# Note: linalg.solve only accepts square matrices in the 1st parameter
np.linalg.solve(A, b)
## array([450., 100., 200.])

The limitation for the 1st parameter to be a square matrix is … quite limiting. There are problems where you could have 20 constraints and 10 decision variables. That’s where np.linalg.lstsq() comes in. Credits to (Halvorsen 2020) for the workaround.

# Removing the 3rd equation to demonstrate finding solution in rectangular matrix
C = np.array([[1, 2, 3],
              [2, 1, 4]])
              
d = np.array([1250, 1800])

# linalg.lstsq for rectangular matrices
np.linalg.lstsq(C, d, rcond = None)[0]
## array([206.57894737,   2.63157895, 346.05263158])
