Operations Research in WWI

During WWI, German U-Boats were an issue for the British as they sunk convoy ships to disrupt supply lines (Editors 2009). Unrestricted submarine warfare took its toll sinking thousands of British vessels. The British Royal Navy have considered implementing the convoy system into action but hesitated due to the potential costs as 1) ships have to travel slowly to maintain the convoy alignment and 2) battleships. However, British scientists came together to find the best way to model their convoy fleets based on ship size, speed, and deployment timing. In addition, the US got involved in WWI roughly 2 years after the RMS Lusitania sinking incident from German U-Boats. As a result, the US provided destroyers to escort those convoys while participating in submarine hunting. The combined forces of optimized British convoys and US naval ships allowed British convoy fatality rates to drop from 10% to 0.5%.

Operations Research in WWII

After WWI, operations research gained more prominence through the development of radar. Originally, the British were looking to construct a death ray to counteract German aircraft (which was deemed impossible). However, when they discovered that those same aircraft was disturbing communication waves, a hypothesis developed of radio waves potentially detecting aircraft. A.P. Rowe’s and Robert Watson-Watt’s breakthrough alongside the assistance of Royal Air Force officers improved the UK’s air defense capabilities by 20-fold. Patrick Blackett, a British physicist, redeployed the convoy system from WWI and tested the UK’s new radar capabilities with great success. The U.S. Air Force eventually decided to have their own operations research groups for wargaming purposes. After WWII, mathematician George B. Dantzig developed the linear programming formulation for the Air Force to optimize planning costs (Leonid Vitalievich Kantorovich from the USSR had the concept earlier although his work wasn’t popular among USSR economists).

Operations Research Post WWII to Today

Operations research has become widespread in the US private sector where over half of US companies have applied it to some capacity. Using mathematical models to find the best solution out of many is a useful skill in any industry. The branding has changed over the years with alias such as:

  • Decision science
  • Management science
  • Analytics
  • Optimization

Nonetheless, the applications that exist today are endless thanks to the advancements in computing power and technology in general.
